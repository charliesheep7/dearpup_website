Your home is full of everyday things that are perfectly harmless to you and genuinely dangerous to your dog. The good news is that serious poisonings are largely preventable once you know what to watch for — and most of the biggest risks come down to a short, memorable list.

Let's walk through the household items that send the most dogs to the vet, the signs of trouble, and exactly what to do if your dog gets into something they shouldn't. No scare tactics — just the practical stuff worth knowing before you ever need it.

## The Short Answer: Know These Six

If you remember nothing else, remember these common household dangers, drawn from the [ASPCA's poison data](https://www.aspca.org/news/official-top-10-toxins-2024):

- **Human medications** — the single biggest category
- **Xylitol** — a sugar substitute hiding in everyday products
- **Chocolate** — especially dark and baking chocolate
- **Grapes and raisins** — tiny amounts, serious risk
- **Onions and garlic** — including powders and leftovers
- **Cleaners and chemicals** — bleach, detergents, antifreeze

## Medications: The Number-One Danger

Human medications top the ASPCA's list of pet poisons, accounting for about **16.5% of all reported exposures**. Over-the-counter drugs — ibuprofen, acetaminophen, cold and flu medicine, and vitamins — are the most common offenders, followed closely by prescription heart medications, antidepressants, and ADHD drugs.

Most cases are heartbreakingly simple: a pill drops on the floor, or a purse with a pill bottle gets left within reach. Keep all medications — yours and your dog's — in closed cabinets, and pick up dropped pills before your dog does.

## Xylitol: The Sneaky One

Xylitol is a sugar substitute that's harmless to people and dangerous to dogs. According to the [ASPCA](https://www.aspca.org/pet-care/aspca-poison-control/poisonous-household-products), in a dog it triggers a sudden insulin release that can crash blood sugar into **hypoglycemia**, and in larger amounts it can cause liver damage. Signs include vomiting, weakness, lethargy, and even seizures.

The tricky part is where it hides: sugarless gum is the classic source, but xylitol also turns up in some sugar-free peanut butters, candy, baked goods, pudding, and toothpaste. **Always check the label before sharing peanut butter with your dog** — that "healthy" sugar-free jar could be the problem.

## Foods Around the House

Plenty of human foods are risky, and a few are outright dangerous:

- **Chocolate** makes up around 13.6% of ASPCA exposures. The darker it is, the more of the toxic compound it contains.
- **Grapes and raisins** can cause acute kidney failure, and there's no reliable "safe" amount — sensitivity varies dog to dog. Symptoms may not show for 24 to 48 hours, so call your vet immediately if your dog eats any.
- **Onions and garlic**, raw, cooked, or powdered, can damage red blood cells over time.

## Cleaners, Chemicals, and Plants

Undiluted **bleach** can injure a dog's mouth and esophagus, and **antifreeze** is especially deadly — its sweet taste makes it dangerously appealing. Store all cleaning products, garage chemicals, and automotive fluids well out of reach, and wipe up spills promptly.

Don't forget greenery. Many common houseplants and garden plants are toxic too — our guide to [poisonous plants for dogs](/blog/poisonous-plants-for-dogs) covers the ones most likely to be in your home.

## Signs Your Dog May Be Poisoned

Symptoms depend on what was ingested, but call your vet if you see:

- Vomiting or diarrhea
- Drooling, or pawing at the mouth
- Weakness, lethargy, or unsteadiness
- Tremors or seizures
- Loss of appetite or unusual behavior

When in doubt, don't wait for symptoms to worsen — early action saves lives.

## What to Do in an Emergency

If you think your dog ate something toxic, stay calm and move quickly:

1. **Remove** any remaining product so your dog can't eat more.
2. **Note** what it was, roughly how much, and when.
3. **Call** your veterinarian or the **ASPCA Animal Poison Control Center at (888) 426-4435** — a 24-hour hotline.
4. **Don't induce vomiting** unless a professional tells you to. With some substances, like bleach, it does more harm than good.

Keep that hotline number somewhere easy to find. In a real emergency, minutes matter, and having the number ready means you're not searching for it while panicking.

## Prevention Is the Whole Game

Dog-proofing your home isn't complicated — it's mostly about height and habit. Store medications and chemicals in closed, elevated cabinets. Keep purses, backpacks, and grocery bags off the floor. Check labels before sharing food. Do a quick scan of any room your dog roams unsupervised.

A few small changes turn your home from a minefield into a safe place for a curious nose. Your dog explores the world with their mouth — your job is just to make sure what they find can't hurt them.
