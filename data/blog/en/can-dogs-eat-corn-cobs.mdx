Summer barbecue, corn on the cob, a dog patrolling under the table — it's a classic setup, and it's exactly how a lot of dogs end up at the emergency vet. Here's the thing worth burning into memory: the corn is fine, but the **cob** is not.

This is one of those cases where a little food is harmless and the container it comes on is genuinely dangerous. Let's break down why, and what to do if your dog gets one.

## Can Dogs Eat Corn Cobs? — The Short Answer

**No — dogs should never eat corn cobs.** Plain corn kernels are safe in moderation, but the cob is a serious hazard. It doesn't digest, and once swallowed it can get stuck in the intestines and cause a life-threatening blockage.

The corn itself isn't toxic. The [AKC](https://www.akc.org/expert-advice/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-corn/) confirms that plain corn kernels are fine for most dogs. The problem is purely mechanical: the cob is the wrong shape and texture to pass safely through a dog's gut.

## Why the Cob Is So Dangerous

A corn cob is dense, fibrous, and woody. Unlike most food, it **doesn't break down in the stomach or intestines** — it stays roughly the same shape the whole way through. Dogs often swallow chunks whole, and a piece just the right size can lodge in the narrow tube of the small intestine.

Once it's stuck, it blocks food and fluid from moving through. That trapped pressure can cut off blood supply to the intestinal wall, and without treatment the tissue can start to die — leading to rupture, severe infection, and shock. This is exactly the kind of gastrointestinal foreign body that the [VCA](https://vcahospitals.com/know-your-pet/ingestion-of-foreign-bodies-in-dogs) calls one of the more common and potentially life-threatening emergencies in dogs, where time matters because a compromised blood supply can cause tissue death within hours.

Small dogs are especially at risk — a cob that a Labrador might pass can completely block a terrier — but a blockage can happen in a dog of any size.

## Signs of a Blockage to Watch For

The tricky part is timing. Symptoms may not show up right away and can develop gradually over **24 to 72 hours**. Watch for:

- Repeated vomiting or retching
- Loss of appetite or refusing food
- Low energy and lethargy
- A hard, painful, or bloated belly, or a hunched posture
- Straining to poop, or no bowel movements

If any of these appear after your dog got near a corn cob, treat it as an emergency.

## What to Do If Your Dog Ate a Corn Cob

Don't wait for symptoms. **Call your vet or an emergency clinic right away**, even if your dog seems completely normal.

1. **Call for advice immediately.** Tell them how much cob, how big your dog is, and when it happened.
2. **Don't try to make your dog vomit at home** unless a vet specifically tells you to — the wrong move can make things worse.
3. **Watch closely** while you get guidance, noting any vomiting, discomfort, or changes in behavior.
4. **Follow through on imaging.** Your vet may want X-rays to see whether the cob is moving or stuck; sometimes small pieces pass with monitoring, and sometimes surgery is needed to remove it.

The reason vets push for an early call is simple: catching a blockage before it damages the intestine is far safer, cheaper, and less invasive than treating a rupture later.

## The Safe Way to Share Corn

Corn on the cob doesn't have to be off-limits at your table — just share it the right way:

- **Cut the kernels off the cob** and offer a small spoonful of plain corn.
- **Skip the butter, salt, and seasonings,** which add fat and sodium your dog doesn't need.
- **Throw cobs straight in a secured trash can,** not an open bin or the compost your dog can raid.
- **Watch the grill and picnic table,** where dropped cobs are easy for a quick dog to grab.
