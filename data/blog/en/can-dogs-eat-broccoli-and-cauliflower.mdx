Chopping vegetables for dinner and wondering if a floret or two is safe to toss to your dog? Broccoli and cauliflower are two of the most common veggies owners ask about — and the good news is both can be a healthy, crunchy treat.

The catch is portion size. These vegetables are great in moderation and a problem in excess. Here's exactly how much is safe and how to serve them.

## The Short Answer

**Yes, dogs can eat broccoli and cauliflower in small amounts.** Neither is toxic, and both add fiber, vitamins, and a satisfying crunch.

The two things to watch are gas and portion size. Both vegetables are high in fiber, and broccoli florets contain a compound that can irritate the stomach in large amounts. Keep servings small and plain, and your dog gets the benefits without the belly ache.

## Are Broccoli and Cauliflower Good for Dogs?

In sensible amounts, these veggies are genuinely nutritious. According to the [AKC](https://www.akc.org/expert-advice/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-broccoli/), broccoli is **high in fiber and vitamin C and low in fat**, making it a healthy occasional treat when served properly.

Cauliflower brings similar perks — fiber, vitamins C and K, and antioxidants — as [PetMD notes](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-cauliflower). For dogs watching their weight, a few low-calorie veggie pieces can be a smart swap for richer treats. They're a good example of the healthy human foods dogs can share; see our roundup of [human foods dogs can eat](/blog/what-human-food-can-dogs-eat) for more.

## The Risks: Gas, Fiber, and the 10% Rule

Here's where portion control matters:

- **Isothiocyanates.** Broccoli florets contain isothiocyanates, which the AKC notes can cause **mild-to-severe gastric irritation** in some dogs if they eat too much.
- **Fiber and gas.** Both vegetables are high in fiber. Overdo it and you'll likely see flatulence, an upset stomach, or diarrhea.
- **Choking hazard.** Thick broccoli stalks and cauliflower cores can be tough and pose a choking or blockage risk, especially for small dogs.

The guideline to remember: broccoli and other treats should stay **under 10% of your dog's daily calories**. The AKC notes that amounts above 25% of daily intake can become genuinely harmful. ## How to Serve Broccoli and Cauliflower Safely

Keep it simple:

1. **Wash thoroughly** to remove pesticides and dirt.
2. **Cut into small, bite-sized pieces** — no large stalks or cores.
3. **Serve plain.** No butter, oil, salt, cheese, and absolutely no garlic or onion, which are toxic to dogs.
4. **Cook to be gentle** on digestion — plain steamed pieces are easiest on the stomach, though small amounts of raw are fine too.
5. **Start small.** Offer a piece or two the first time and watch how your dog reacts before making it a regular treat.

## How Much Is Right for Your Dog's Size

The 10% rule is the guardrail, but the actual amount depends on your dog's size. A tiny floret goes a long way for a Chihuahua, while a Labrador can handle a bit more without issue.

- **Small dogs (under 20 lbs):** one or two small, chopped pieces.
- **Medium dogs (20–50 lbs):** a small handful of bite-sized pieces.
- **Large dogs (50+ lbs):** a modest handful, still well under 10% of the day's food.

When you introduce any new vegetable, start at the low end and see how your dog's stomach responds before offering more. Broccoli and cauliflower aren't the only veggies worth trying, either — plain, cooked corn kernels are another common one owners ask about, and our guide on whether [dogs can eat corn](/blog/can-dogs-eat-corn) covers the details.

## When to Call Your Vet

A little gas after a veggie treat is normal. But contact your vet if, after eating broccoli or cauliflower, your dog shows:

- Persistent vomiting or diarrhea
- Signs of choking, gagging, or trouble swallowing
- Severe bloating or obvious abdominal pain
- Lethargy or a refusal to eat

These are uncommon with small servings, but they're worth a call — especially if your dog got into a large amount.

Bottom line: broccoli and cauliflower are a healthy, low-calorie treat your dog can enjoy — just keep the portions small, the prep plain, and the pieces bite-sized.
