## The Month That Opens the Islamic Year

محرم (_Muharram_) is the first month of the Islamic lunar calendar. Its name means "forbidden" or "sacred" — and unlike any other month of the year, the Prophet ﷺ singled it out by name as شَهْرُ اللَّهِ الْمُحَرَّمُ — "the sacred month of Allah."

If you have ever wondered why some Muslims fast in what seems like an ordinary month, or why عاشوراء (_Ashura_) carries such weight in Islamic tradition, this is where those answers begin. Muharram and Ashura are not obscure rituals — they are an annual invitation to connect with prophetic history, earn immense reward, and reset your intentions for the new Islamic year.

## What Muharram and Ashura Actually Mean

### Muharram: One of the Four Sacred Months

Muharram is one of four months Allah designated as sacred — alongside Rajab, Dhul-Qadah, and Dhul-Hijjah. Our companion guide on [the four sacred months in Islam](/blog/the-four-sacred-months-in-islam) explains the full picture of why these months matter and what distinguishes them.

The Prophet ﷺ described Muharram's standing clearly:

> "The best fasting after Ramadan is the month of Allah, Muharram."
> — ([Sahih Muslim 1163](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1163))

Calling it "the month of Allah" is a significant honor in Islamic texts. Only a handful of things are attributed to Allah in this possessive way — and this month is among them. That alone tells you something about its weight.

### Ashura: The Day of the Tenth

The word _Ashura_ simply means "the tenth" — the 10th day of Muharram. This day is anchored in one of the most moving moments in prophetic history.

When the Prophet ﷺ arrived in Medina after the Hijrah, he observed Jewish communities fasting on this day. He asked about it and was told:

> "This is a great day on which Allah saved Musa and drowned Pharaoh, so Musa fasted this day in gratitude to Allah."

The Prophet ﷺ responded: "We have more right to Musa than they do." He fasted Ashura and instructed his Companions to do the same.
— ([Sahih al-Bukhari 2004](https://sunnah.com/bukhari:2004), [Sahih Muslim 1130](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1130))

This moment reveals something beautiful about Islam: the Prophet ﷺ did not see the connection to [Musa (peace be upon him)](/blog/who-was-prophet-musa) as a foreign tradition to avoid. He embraced it as part of the shared chain of prophecy — a reminder that the ummah's spiritual inheritance stretches back through every prophet sent by Allah.

### The Reward for Fasting Ashura

The Prophet ﷺ was asked about fasting on Ashura. He replied:

> "It expiates the sins of the previous year."
> — ([Sahih Muslim 1162](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1162))

One day of fasting, one year of sins expiated. Among voluntary acts of worship, Ashura offers one of the most generous returns in the entire Islamic calendar. If you fast nothing else voluntarily throughout the year, this is the one to prioritize.

## Why Muharram Matters for Muslims Today

We live in a culture that marks new years with countdowns and resolutions — and then moves on by February. The Islamic new year works differently. The 1st of Muharram marks the beginning of the Hijri year, but the focus is not on celebration. It is on worship, reflection, and intention.

This is actually freeing. There is no social pressure, no prescribed gathering, no grand gesture required. The month begins quietly, and what you do with it is between you and Allah.

Some practical realities worth knowing:

- **There is no specific prayer prescribed for 1 Muharram.** There is no authentic hadith calling for a special gathering or ritual to mark the new Islamic year. Be cautious of practices presented as obligatory for this occasion.
- **The emphasis is on voluntary worship throughout the month** — with Ashura as the centerpiece.
- **It is a natural moment to reset your habits.** Many Muslims use the new Hijri year to renew their intentions for salah, [istighfar](/blog/benefits-of-istighfar), and charitable giving.

## How to Honor Muharram and Ashura

### Fasting on Ashura and Around It

The minimum is fasting the 10th of Muharram. Before the Prophet ﷺ passed away, he indicated an intention to go further:

> "If I am alive next year, I will certainly fast on the ninth."
> — ([Sahih Muslim 1134](https://sunnah.com/muslim:1134))

The wisdom: by adding the 9th, Muslims distinguish their observance from that of the Jewish communities, while retaining the full spiritual benefit of Ashura. Scholars offer three valid options:

- **Fast the 9th and 10th** — this is what the Prophet ﷺ intended and is the most recommended
- **Fast the 10th alone** — valid and rewarded
- **Fast the 10th and 11th** — also mentioned by some scholars as an alternative

Missing Ashura is not a sin — it is a voluntary act. But given the reward, it is worth building into your calendar well in advance.

### Increasing Dhikr Through the Month

[The importance of dhikr](/blog/the-importance-of-dhikr) is amplified during sacred months. A simple daily addition that fits into any schedule:

> سُبْحَانَ اللَّهِ — _Subhanallah_ (Glory be to Allah)
>
> الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ — _Alhamdulillah_ (All praise is for Allah)
>
> اللَّهُ أَكْبَرُ — _Allahu Akbar_ (Allah is the Greatest)

One hundred of each after your daily prayers through Muharram is a practical, achievable goal. In a sacred month, these small acts carry greater weight.

### Giving Sadaqah in Muharram

Generosity during sacred months is especially encouraged. Even modest acts of sadaqah count as heightened acts of worship in Muharram. Consider:

- Giving a regular daily amount to a reliable charity throughout the month
- Feeding someone who is fasting on Ashura
- Settling any financial obligations you have been putting off

### Reflecting on the New Hijri Year

The start of Muharram is a natural moment to take stock: What did the past year teach me? What do I want to build in this new Hijri year? Growing in [taqwa — God-consciousness](/blog/what-is-taqwa-in-islam) — does not have a calendar deadline, but a new year is a real prompt to recalibrate. ## Signs of Spiritual Growth in Muharram

You do not need a dramatic transformation to know the month is working on you. These are signs that Muharram is quietly doing its job:

- Ashura is on your calendar before the month arrives — it is part of your year, not a last-minute thought
- You fast on Ashura, even if it is your first time
- Your salah feels more consistent through the month
- You find yourself making _istighfar_ naturally, without forcing it

These are not signs of perfection. They are signs of awareness — and sincerity in worship is exactly what Allah looks for.

## Common Questions About Muharram and Ashura

**Is Ashura a day of mourning?**

Sunni Muslims observe Ashura primarily as a day of voluntary fasting and gratitude for Allah's salvation of Musa. It is a day of worship, not mourning. Shia Muslims additionally observe it as the day of the martyrdom of Husayn ibn Ali (may Allah have mercy on him) at Karbala in 61 AH — a significant historical tragedy that occurred decades after the Prophet's ﷺ death. Sunni scholars hold that the mourning practices associated with Karbala are not grounded in authentic hadith from the Prophet ﷺ.

**Does fasting Ashura replace any obligatory fast?**

No. Ashura is a voluntary fast. It does not replace missed days from Ramadan, which must be made up separately. Ashura's expiation of a year's sins refers to minor sins; major sins require sincere repentance (_tawbah_) beyond fasting alone.

**What if I am not able to fast on Ashura this year?**

Missing Ashura carries no sin or penalty. Increase other acts of worship in Muharram instead — dhikr, Quran recitation, sadaqah — and make an intention to observe it next year. The Quran reminds us that [Allah does not burden a soul beyond what it can bear](https://quran.com/2/286) (Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:286), and every sincere intention is counted.

**Is there a special dua for Muharram or Ashura?**

There is no specific dua prescribed by the Prophet ﷺ for Muharram or Ashura. You can make any sincere personal dua asking Allah to bless the new year, accept your fasts, and guide you toward growth. Sincerity in general dua during sacred months is especially valued.

## Closing Thoughts

Muharram arrives without fanfare. There is no countdown, no decorations, no cultural pressure. But within it sits one of the most quietly generous days in the Islamic year — Ashura, when a single day of fasting expiates a year of sins.

Start simply: put Ashura on your calendar. Fast it. Pair it with the 9th if you can. Increase your dhikr throughout the month. Let the new Hijri year begin with at least one act of intentional worship rather than simply another date on the calendar.

That is all Muharram asks of you — and that is entirely within your reach.
