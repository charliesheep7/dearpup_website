Every morning begins with a moment you often rush past. Eyes open, thoughts flood in — what needs to get done, what yesterday left unfinished — and before you know it, the morning is half gone without a word said to Allah.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ left us something for that exact moment between sleeping and rising: a short supplication, ten words in Arabic. In those ten words, you acknowledge that sleep is a kind of death, that waking up is a resurrection, and that the day ahead belongs entirely to Allah.

This is the waking up dua — and once you know it, you will never think of mornings the same way.

## What Is the Dua for Waking Up?

The waking up dua, recorded in both Sahih al-Bukhari 6312 and Sahih Muslim 2711, is: _Alhamdulillāhi alladhī aḥyānā ba'da mā amātanā wa ilayhin-nushūr_ — "All praise is for Allah who gave us life after having taken it from us, and unto Him is the resurrection." The Prophet ﷺ taught this supplication to be said immediately upon opening one's eyes, before speaking or rising, making it the day's first act of worship.

## The Complete Waking Up Dua

The primary waking up dua from the Sunnah:

> اَلْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ الَّذِي أَحْيَانَا بَعْدَ مَا أَمَاتَنَا وَإِلَيْهِ النُّشُورُ
>
> _Alhamdulillāhi alladhī aḥyānā ba'da mā amātanā wa ilayhin-nushūr_
>
> "All praise is for Allah who gave us life after having taken it from us, and unto Him is the resurrection."
> — (Sahih al-Bukhari 6312; Sahih Muslim 2711)

**When to recite it:** Immediately upon waking — before rising from your bed, before reaching for your phone, before speaking to anyone. _Ḥīna yastayyiqiẓ_ — "upon waking" — is how the hadith describes the timing.

The dua can be read silently or aloud. If you wake during the night for Tahajjud and go back to sleep, you may recite it again when you rise for the day. The goal is to make the first conscious act of the day a remembrance of Allah.

There is also a longer night-waking dua, suitable for rising specifically for _Qiyam al-Layl_:

> لَا إِلَهَ إِلَّا اللَّهُ وَحْدَهُ لَا شَرِيكَ لَهُ، لَهُ الْمُلْكُ وَلَهُ الْحَمْدُ، وَهُوَ عَلَى كُلِّ شَيْءٍ قَدِيرٌ
>
> _Lā ilāha illAllāhu waḥdahu lā sharīka lahu, lahul-mulku wa lahul-ḥamdu wa huwa 'alā kulli shay'in qadīr_
>
> "There is no god but Allah, alone without partner. His is the dominion, His is all praise, and He has power over all things."
> — (Sahih al-Bukhari 1154)

When followed by sincere dua, the Prophet ﷺ taught that this recitation brings forgiveness — making night-waking one of the most spiritually potent moments available to a believer.

## Why Sleep Is Called a "Minor Death" in Islam

The Islamic understanding of sleep gives the waking up dua its full weight. Allah says:

> "Allah takes the souls at the time of their death, and those that do not die [He takes] during their sleep. Then He keeps those for which He has decreed death and releases the others for a specified term."
> — (Surah Az-Zumar, 39:42; [quran.com/39/42](https://quran.com/39/42))

Every night, your soul is in Allah's hands. Waking up is not a guarantee — it is a mercy. The Prophet ﷺ reinforced this through the [dua for sleeping](/blog/dua-for-sleeping): _Bismika Allahumma amutu wa ahya_ — "In Your name, O Allah, I die and I live" — and through the waking up dua, which mirrors it each morning.

This is why the dua opens with praise (_alhamdulillāh_) rather than a request. You wake owing gratitude, not making demands. Allah has already given you the most fundamental thing you could receive: another day of life.

The word _nushūr_ (نُشُور), translated as "resurrection," connects every morning to the ultimate awakening. Each new day is a rehearsal for the Day of Judgment, when all souls will be raised. Reciting this dua keeps that perspective alive.

See [the importance of dhikr](/blog/the-importance-of-dhikr) for more on why remembrance shapes the quality of every hour that follows.

## How to Make the Waking Up Dua a Daily Habit

Knowing the dua is one thing. Saying it consistently — half-awake, before your alarm has fully registered — takes intention. Here is how to build it without relying on willpower alone.

**Habit-stack it before getting out of bed.** The moment you become conscious, recite the dua before your feet hit the floor. This works because the trigger (waking) is non-negotiable — the dua slides in before any other behavior can compete.

**Place the text where you wake up.** A small card on your nightstand with the Arabic and transliteration acts as a visual cue until the words are memorized. Many Muslims find that seeing the Arabic script — even before they can read it — prompts recitation.

**Link it to Fajr.** The waking up dua and [Fajr prayer](/blog/fajr-prayer-benefits) are natural companions. Scholars recommend reciting the dua, making wudu, and beginning the [morning adhkar](/blog/morning-adhkar-in-islam) as a single unbroken chain before the Fajr window closes. Learning [how to pray Fajr](/blog/how-to-pray-fajr) alongside these morning practices strengthens the habit loop.

**Teach it to your household.** When children and family members also recite the waking up dua, the entire home begins its day in remembrance of Allah — and the habit becomes self-reinforcing through community.

## Common Questions About the Waking Up Dua

### Does the waking up dua need to be said in Arabic?

Arabic is strongly encouraged because the Prophet ﷺ recited the dua in Arabic. Start with the English translation until you have memorized the Arabic — both the intention and meaning matter, but aligning with the Prophetic practice is the goal. Most people find the Arabic memorized within a week of daily recitation.

### Can I say the waking up dua if I have not made wudu yet?

Yes. The waking up dua does not require wudu. It is a general remembrance (_dhikr_), not a ritual prayer requiring purity. You can say it the moment you open your eyes — wudu comes next in the morning sequence.

### What if I wake up multiple times in the night?

Recite the dua each time you wake intending to return to sleep. If you are rising specifically for Tahajjud, the longer night-waking dua (Sahih al-Bukhari 1154) is especially recommended, as it carries the promise of forgiveness when followed by sincere supplication.

### Is there a separate waking up dua for Fajr time?

The standard waking up dua applies whenever you rise. For the Fajr period specifically, scholars recommend following it with the morning adhkar — a prescribed collection of remembrances for after _Fajr_ has been prayed. The Quranic context of Surah Az-Zumar 39:42 is worth reading as foundation.

### How long does it take to memorize the waking up dua?

The waking up dua is 12 words in Arabic. Most people memorize it within three to five days of reciting it each morning with the transliteration card nearby. Begin by reading it, then try to recite from memory before checking — the active recall accelerates retention.

## Closing

The waking up dua asks for nothing. It offers praise. And that is exactly where a Muslim's day should begin — not with requests, but with gratitude for the gift of another morning.

Ten words. Ten seconds. The foundation of a day that remembers Allah.

Make it your first habit. Recite it before your feet touch the floor. Then watch how a small act of remembrance changes the quality of every hour that follows — as the Sunnah has always promised it would.
