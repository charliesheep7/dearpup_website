You're plating up salmon or peeling shrimp, and a hopeful face appears at your side. Can dogs eat seafood, or is it one of those foods best kept to yourself?

The short version: a lot of seafood is perfectly safe for dogs — but *how* you prepare it makes all the difference. Here's the vet-informed rundown on what's safe, what to skip, and how to share seafood without any regrets.

## The Short Answer

**Yes, dogs can eat many kinds of seafood — as long as it's fully cooked, plain, boneless, and served in moderation.** Cooked fish and properly prepared shellfish can be a healthy, protein-rich treat.

The risks come from four things: raw seafood, high-mercury fish, bones and shells, and added fats or seasonings. Avoid those, and seafood is a great occasional addition to your dog's bowl.

## Why Seafood Can Be Good for Dogs

Fish is a staple ingredient in many quality dog foods for good reason. According to the [AKC](https://www.akc.org/expert-advice/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-fish/), fish is a **great source of lean protein, is low in saturated fat, and delivers omega-3 fatty acids** that support a healthy skin, coat, and joints.

Shellfish like shrimp add protein plus nutrients such as vitamin B12 and antioxidants. In sensible amounts, seafood can be a nutritious change of pace from the usual treats.

That said, seafood is an *extra*, not a dietary requirement. Your dog gets everything they need from a complete, balanced food, so think of a bite of cooked fish as a healthy bonus rather than a meal replacement — and let your dog's size, weight, and activity level guide how much is reasonable.

## Seafood That's Generally Safe (Cooked and Plain)

When fully cooked and served without seasoning, these are commonly dog-friendly:

- **Fish** — salmon, whitefish, cod, flounder, and similar. See our full guide on whether [dogs can eat fish](/blog/can-dogs-eat-fish).
- **Shrimp** — cooked, with the shell, tail, and vein removed. More detail in our [can dogs eat shrimp](/blog/can-dogs-eat-shrimp) guide.
- **Tilapia** — a mild, low-mercury whitefish; details in [can dogs eat tilapia](/blog/can-dogs-eat-tilapia).
- **Scallops** — plain and cooked, in small amounts ([can dogs eat scallops](/blog/can-dogs-eat-scallops)).
- **Lobster and oysters** — only occasionally and carefully; see [can dogs eat lobster](/blog/can-dogs-eat-lobster) for the specifics.

The common thread: cooked, plain, and portion-controlled.

## Seafood and Prep to Be Careful With

Some seafood situations carry real risks:

- **Raw fish and shellfish.** The [AVMA does not recommend raw or undercooked fish](https://www.akc.org/expert-advice/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-fish/) for dogs of any age because of parasites and bacteria like salmonella, Listeria, and Clostridium. Skip the sushi.
- **High-mercury fish.** Long-lived fish such as tuna and swordfish can accumulate mercury, which builds up over time. Occasional small amounts are usually fine; frequent large servings are not.
- **Bones.** Fish bones are small, brittle, and dangerous — they can lodge in or even perforate the mouth, throat, or gut. Always debone.
- **Shells, tails, and legs.** Shrimp shells and crab legs are choking and blockage hazards. Remove them entirely.
- **Butter, oil, salt, and seasonings.** As [PetMD notes for shrimp](https://www.petmd.com/dog/nutrition/can-dogs-eat-shrimp), you should skip added fats and spices — and **garlic and onion are toxic to dogs**, so never serve seafood cooked with them.

## How to Serve Seafood Safely

Keep it simple and your dog can enjoy seafood without drama:

1. **Cook it thoroughly** — no raw, no undercooked.
2. **Remove every bone, shell, tail, and vein.**
3. **Serve it plain** — no butter, oil, salt, garlic, onion, or sauce.
4. **Start small** — introduce a bite or two and watch for any reaction, especially since shellfish can trigger allergies.
5. **Keep it occasional** — treats and extras should stay under 10% of daily calories.

## When to Call Your Vet

Most seafood mishaps are minor, but watch for these signs and call your vet if they appear after your dog eats seafood:

- Vomiting, diarrhea, or loss of appetite
- Signs of choking, gagging, or pawing at the mouth
- Itching, swelling, hives, or facial puffiness (possible allergic reaction)
- Lethargy or any behavior that seems off

When in doubt, your vet is always the right call — especially if your dog swallowed bones or shells. Shellfish allergies, while uncommon, are worth taking seriously: if you notice itching or swelling after a first taste of shrimp or crab, stop offering it and check with your vet before trying again.

Seafood can absolutely have a place in your dog's diet. Cook it, keep it plain, remove the risky bits, and serve it in moderation — and that hopeful face at your side gets a healthy, happy treat.
